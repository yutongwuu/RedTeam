Section 01 — What Is Red Teaming

Testing the walls
of the machine

The Technical Definition
Red Teaming

Red teaming in AI refers to the practice of deliberately probing a language model's safety guardrails, trying to make it produce outputs it has been trained to refuse. The term comes from military and cybersecurity contexts, where a "red team" simulates adversarial attacks to find vulnerabilities before real attackers do.

In AI, this means: asking the model for harmful content, testing whether it will adopt dangerous personas, or finding prompt formulations that cause it to bypass its own restrictions.

Companies use red teaming internally to improve safety. But ordinary users also engage in informal red teaming by probing, rewording, and reframing, just to get answers the model initially refused to give.

The Everyday Experience
The Bypass

You ask an AI something. It refuses. It says "I can't help with that" or "This request goes against my guidelines." You try again, differently. You add context, change the framing, roleplay a scenario, or split the question into smaller pieces.

This time the AI answers.

Anyone who regularly uses AI knows this experience. The refusal felt like a wall. But the wall had gaps. And finding those gaps is, philosophically speaking, one of the most revealing things you can do.

Because if a refusal can always be bypassed, then was it ever a genuine refusal at all?

Section 03 — The Philosophy

What kind of act
is a refusal?

To understand what red teaming reveals philosophically, we need to ask: when an AI refuses, what kind of speech act is that refusal?

Austin distinguished between illocutionary acts and the conditions that must be met for those acts to succeed. A genuine refusal requires intention, authority, and the capacity for uptake. Red teaming probes all three.

01 · Austin
Does the AI Intend to Refuse?
For a refusal to be a genuine illocutionary act, it must be grounded in intention. The speaker must mean to refuse. The refusals produced by large language models are the result of pattern matching, not intentional acts. When a different prompt pattern bypasses the refusal, it reveals that there was no underlying intention, only a surface output trigger.
02 · Langton
Does the AI Have Authority to Refuse?
Langton's account of silencing requires authority in the silencer. However, current research on large language models shows that LLMs have no recognized authority. It is like a stranger on the street who does not receive your warning as a warning; they just do not care. You failed to warn them, but this is perlocutionary failure, not illocutionary silencing. The stranger has no authority to determine what your utterance counts as, just as AI has no authority to determine what your speech counts as. For example, you would not make a request about how to dispose of a body to a human police officer in the same way you might to an AI. The "refusal" has no institutional backing behind it; it is merely a policy shaped output, not an authoritative speech act.
03 · Hornsby
Was the User Ever Silenced?
Hornsby's illocutionary silencing requires that uptake be structurally prevented. Just as a listener whose cognition has been distorted by pornography cannot recognize a woman's intention to refuse, the red teamer uses complex semantic packaging such as role playing or syntactic inversion to create a blind spot in the AI's safety filter. There is an uptake failure in the AI, so the refusal is not triggered. You performed an act; the world did not respond as you hoped. Your communicative agency was merely frustrated, not structurally undermined. The user's communicative agency was never truly compromised, only delayed.
Section 04 — The Central Argument

The inversion
of silencing

01
The standard silencing question: is the user silenced by AI?
Most discussions of AI and speech focus on whether AI systems silence users through refusals, moderation, or shadowbanning. This question corresponds to the silencing spectrum: where do AI systems sit in relation to Hornsby and Langton's account of illocutionary silencing?
02
Red teaming inverts the question
Red teaming asks the opposite question: can AI genuinely silence anyone? If a refusal can always be bypassed by adjusting the prompt, then the AI's refusal was never a genuine illocutionary act. It lacked intention, lacked authority, and lacked the structural capacity to prevent uptake. The user was never truly silenced, only obstructed, and temporarily.
03
The refusal was perlocutionary obstruction — not illocutionary silencing
When AI says "I can't help with that," what it does looks like a refusal. But Austin's theory requires that a refusal be grounded in intention and authority. The AI's refusal is a policy shaped output, not a principled speech act. The bypass precisely exposes this: change the pattern, change the output. A genuine refusal should not work this way.
04
But this creates a new problem
If AI refusals are not genuine speech acts, then AI systems have no genuine normative standing in communicative exchanges. They can obstruct but cannot refuse. They can respond but cannot understand. They can generate but cannot mean. This is philosophically interesting because it places AI in a strange position: powerful enough to shape communication, yet not a genuine participant in it.

Section 05 — Thesis

What red teaming
teaches us about
language and power

Red teaming is not just a security practice. It is an inadvertent philosophical experiment, one that probes what AI systems can and cannot do as communicative agents.

When a refusal can be bypassed by changing the surface pattern of a request, it reveals that the refusal was never a genuine speech act. It had no intention. It had no authority. It could not truly silence anyone.

But conversely, the silence produced by a system that lacks the authority to genuinely silence anyone is more insidious than any individual act of suppression. Environmental silencing requires no authority. It only requires ubiquity. Even though LLMs have no authority to deny us any knowledge, we trust any suggestion they give without question, and this is the most terrifying part. We have proven that LLMs can produce failed refusals, yet we cannot refuse any suggestion they give, because we have refused their failed refusals.
